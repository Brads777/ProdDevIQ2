2   Capstone Report

The capstone report is the centerpiece of your portfolio. 8–12 pages, professional formatting. This is not a data dump — it is a polished narrative that tells the story of your product from inception to market validation.

1 Executive Summary (1 page)

The product, the market, the verdict. A busy executive should understand your product after reading only this page. Cover what the product is, who it serves, how you validated it, and whether it is ready for market.

2 The Journey (2–3 pages)

How you got here. Key decisions at each phase, pivots you made, what surprised you. This is not a chronological log — it is a narrative of the most important moments. Focus on:

  • The critical decision points where your product could have gone a different direction
  • Pivots you made and what motivated them
  • What you believed at the start versus what the data told you
  • Moments where the team disagreed and how you resolved it

3 Market Validation Results (2–3 pages)

Your Phase 3 test marketing findings presented with professional rigor:

  • Persona panel composition: Demographics, segments, diversity of the testing panel
  • Scoring results: Before and after iteration — show the improvement
  • Key objections: What the personas pushed back on and how you addressed each one
  • Segment analysis: Which segments are hot versus cold and why
  • Data visualizations: Include charts, heat maps, and graphs — not just raw numbers

4 Product Specification (1–2 pages)

Final product description incorporating all changes from testing. This is your product-requirements-v2.md in polished prose, not raw markdown. Write it as if a developer or manufacturer will build from this description. Include core features, target user, key differentiators, and technical requirements.

5 Go-to-Market Readiness Assessment (1 page)

An honest evaluation of where your product stands. Answer these questions directly:

  • Is this product ready for real users? Why or why not?
  • What would you need to do next to launch?
  • What gaps remain in the product, the research, or the team’s capabilities?
  • What is the biggest risk if you launched tomorrow?

6 Reflection (1–2 pages)

Each team member contributes a personal reflection (300–500 words each). This section should contain clearly labeled individual entries. Each person addresses:

  • What would you do differently if you started over?
  • Do you feel this product is ready to go to market? Why or why not?
  • What other steps would have helped you prepare better?
  • What was the most valuable thing you learned in this course?
★ Quality bar: Think of this report as something you would include in a job portfolio or show to an investor. Professional formatting, clear writing, evidence-based arguments. First impressions matter — a polished report signals a polished thinker.

3   Final Presentation Video

Up to 15 minutes. Polished, rehearsed, professional. This is NOT a screen recording of you reading slides — it is a presentation you would give to investors or stakeholders. Record it with good audio, proper lighting, and confident delivery.

SegmentDurationContent
The Problem & Opportunity 2 min What problem did you discover? Why does it matter? Who suffers from it?
The Product 3 min What you built. Live demo or walkthrough. Key features and differentiators.
The Research Journey 3 min Phase 1 highlights: strategic brief findings, LLM Council debate, key pivots from research to refined concept.
Market Validation 3 min Phase 3 results: what 100–200 personas said, how you iterated, final scores and verdict.

⚠ Non-negotiable: Every team member must speak in the video. Label who is speaking with a name overlay or verbal introduction at each transition. A presentation where one person carries the entire team is not a team presentation.

4   Product Demo

The format of your product demo depends on what you built. Follow the guidelines for your product type.

If your product is a software application:

  • Record a 3–5 minute screen recording walking through the working prototype
  • Show the core user flow from start to finish
  • Demonstrate at least 3 key features
  • Show it working (deployed URL preferred, localhost acceptable)

If your product is a physical object:

  • Provide 5–10 high-resolution photos from multiple angles
  • Include at least one photo showing the product in use (in context)
  • Include a 1-page product spec sheet (dimensions, materials, manufacturing considerations)

If your product is a business concept / service:

  • Brand identity package: logo, color palette, typography
  • Service flow diagram showing how a customer interacts with the business
  • At least 2 mockups showing customer touchpoints (website, app, physical space, etc.)
  • A 1-page business model canvas
Regardless of product type: Your demo should demonstrate that the product works or has been thought through to a level of detail that proves feasibility. Concepts alone are not enough — show evidence of execution.

7a   How to Build Your Slides and Record Your Video

This section walks you through the two most common questions: how to generate professional slides using Claude Code, and how to record a polished demo video.

Generating Slides with the pptx Skill

Claude Code can generate a complete PowerPoint deck directly from your data. This is the fastest way to turn your Phase 3 results, capstone insights, and product narrative into presentation-ready slides.

Step-by-step:

  1. Open Claude Code in your project directory (where your Phase 1–3 artifacts live).
  2. Type “Create a presentation for my final deliverable” or run /pptx to invoke the PowerPoint skill directly.
  3. Claude will read your artifacts (product-requirements.md, persona-validation-results.md, market-heat-map.md, etc.) and generate a .pptx file with your content organized into slides.
  4. Tell Claude what you want. Example prompts:
    • “Create a 15-slide presentation covering problem, product, research journey, market validation results, and go-to-market recommendation.”
    • “Include a slide showing my persona panel demographics as a pie chart.”
    • “Add a before-and-after slide comparing my Round 1 and retest scores.”
    • “Generate a market heat map slide showing which segments are hot vs. cold.”
  5. Open the generated .pptx in PowerPoint or Google Slides. Customize colors, fonts, and images to match your brand.
  6. Iterate: ask Claude to regenerate specific slides or add charts from your validation data.
★ Pro tip: Don’t try to fit everything on every slide. The best presentations have one idea per slide with supporting data. Let your spoken narration carry the detail — the slides are visual anchors, not a script.

Recording Your Demo Video

Your product demo video (3–5 minutes, separate from the presentation video) should show your product in action. Here’s how, by product type:

Software applications:

  1. Screen recording tools (all free):
    • Windows: Press Win + G to open Xbox Game Bar → click Record. Or use OBS Studio (free, cross-platform).
    • Mac: Press Cmd + Shift + 5 for built-in screen recording. Or use OBS Studio.
    • Loom (free tier): Records screen + webcam with instant sharing link.
  2. Before you hit record:
    • Close unnecessary tabs and notifications
    • Set your browser zoom to 100% (or 90% if your app is wide)
    • Pre-load your app to the starting screen so you don’t waste time navigating
    • Write a brief script: “First I’ll show [feature A], then [feature B], then [feature C]”
  3. Narrate as you go. Explain what you’re clicking and why. “Here I’m creating a new [X] — notice how the interface [Y].”
  4. Show the complete user flow from first interaction to final outcome. Don’t skip steps.

Physical products:

  1. Use a clean, well-lit surface (natural light near a window works best).
  2. Take photos from 5+ angles: front, back, side, top-down, and at least one in-context shot (product being used or in its intended environment).
  3. Use your phone camera in landscape mode at the highest resolution.
  4. For a video walkthrough: hold the camera steady (use a tripod or prop your phone) and narrate the product’s features as you show them.

Business concepts / services:

  1. Use the pptx skill to generate your brand identity slides (logo, color palette, typography choices).
  2. Use Claude Code or Canva (free for students) to create service flow diagrams and customer touchpoint mockups.
  3. Record a narrated walkthrough of your mockups using screen recording, showing how a customer moves through the service experience.

Combining Everything into the Final Presentation Video

Your 15-minute final video combines your slides, demo footage, and team narration. Here’s the recommended workflow:

  1. Generate your slide deck using /pptx in Claude Code.
  2. Record your demo separately (3–5 minutes).
  3. Record each team member’s section with slides visible (share screen on Zoom/Teams/Google Meet and record, or use Loom).
  4. Edit together using a free video editor:
    • CapCut (free, very easy, browser-based)
    • DaVinci Resolve (free, professional-grade)
    • iMovie (free on Mac)
    • Clipchamp (built into Windows 11)
  5. Add name overlays for each speaker transition so the viewer knows who is talking.
  6. Export as MP4 at 1080p. Upload to Canvas or provide a shareable link (Google Drive, YouTube unlisted).
Recording tip: Record each speaker’s segment separately, then stitch them together. This is far easier than trying to get a perfect 15-minute take. You can also record as a team on Zoom/Teams — just make sure each person shares their screen when presenting their section.

9   Tips for Success

  • Start the capstone report NOW. Do not wait until the last weekend. Writing 8–12 quality pages takes multiple drafts. First drafts are never good enough.
  • Record the presentation video early. Bad audio, poor lighting, and rushed delivery are the most common issues. Record a practice run first. Watch it back. Then record the real one.
  • Every team member must speak in the video. This is non-negotiable. Plan who covers which section and rehearse the transitions.
  • Your product demo should work. If your prototype has bugs, fix them or clearly note known issues. Do not pretend it works when it does not.
  • Use your Phase 3 data in the presentation. “73% of personas said X” is more persuasive than “we think people will like it.” Let the evidence do the talking.
  • The reflection is personal. Do not copy-paste the same reflection across team members. The grader will notice. Each person’s 300–500 words should be distinctly their own.
  • Organize your submission folder cleanly. A messy submission suggests a messy process. Follow the folder structure in Section 6 exactly.
  • Submit the peer evaluation separately and honestly. It is confidential and it matters. Do not skip it — missing peer evaluations result in a grade penalty.
  • Proofread everything. Typos in a capstone report undermine your credibility. Read it out loud. Have a teammate review it. Run spell check at minimum.
  • This portfolio could go on your resume or LinkedIn. Treat it accordingly. The best student portfolios are indistinguishable from professional work.
