## 1. What You Are Doing

You have spent nine weeks building a product from scratch — from strategic brief to working prototype. Now it is time to find out if anyone would actually buy it.

This week, you will subject your prototype and PRD to **rigorous simulated market testing** using **100–200 AI-generated synthetic personas**. These personas are demographically diverse, psychographically distinct, and programmed to give honest — sometimes harsh — feedback. This is **not** a rubber stamp. It is a stress test.

The goal is simple: **find the weaknesses before you present to real stakeholders.**

You will conduct three types of testing:

### 1. Simulated Market Testing — 100–200 Personas

A large panel of AI-generated personas evaluates your concept and provides purchase intent scores, top objections, and detailed feedback. Each persona has a unique demographic profile, tech savviness level, pain point intensity, and willingness to pay. The panel produces a statistically meaningful picture of how your target market would respond.

### 2. Simulated User Interviews — 5 Representative Personas

Five carefully selected personas from the panel sit for deep, 10-question one-on-one interviews. These explore current workflows, pain points, reactions to your prototype, willingness to switch from existing solutions, concerns, and concrete suggestions.

### 3. Usability Testing Simulation — UI/UX Heuristic Evaluation

Personas evaluate your prototype's user experience against standard usability heuristics, revealing where the interface confuses, frustrates, or delights different user types.

**How many personas?** The count depends on your target market breadth:

| Market Type | Persona Count |
|---|---|
| Narrow or highly specific market (e.g., pediatric dentists in urban areas) | 100 personas |
| Broad or diverse market (e.g., all small business owners) | 200 personas |

**You MUST iterate.** Run the validation, improve your PRD based on the feedback, and retest until you achieve a **"Proceed" verdict** (above 80% composite score). If you cannot reach "Proceed" after two retests, document why and present your plan for getting there.

## 2. Apply Critical Thinking to Your Test Marketing Results

Simulated market data is only as valuable as your ability to interpret it. Use these five critical thinking skills to move beyond surface-level observations and extract real insight from your persona feedback.

| Thinking Skill | What It Means | Apply to Your Test Results |
|---|---|---|
| **Analysis** | Break complex results into component parts | Segment persona feedback by demographic. Identify which user types love the concept and which hate it. Find patterns in objections — are price concerns concentrated in one income bracket or spread evenly? |
| **Evaluation** | Assess credibility and relevance of evidence | Not all persona feedback is equal. Weight responses by how closely each persona matches your primary target customer. A skeptic outside your target saying "I wouldn't buy this" means less than your ideal customer saying "I have concerns." Distinguish "nice to have" from "must fix." |
| **Inference** | Draw warranted conclusions from evidence | Connect persona objections to specific product decisions. If 60% of personas cite price as their top objection, the issue is not your marketing copy — it is your business model. Follow the data to root causes. |
| **Self-Regulation** | Monitor your own reasoning and bias | Resist the urge to dismiss negative feedback. If you find yourself explaining away objections ("those personas just don't get it"), that is a signal to investigate deeper, not to ignore. Check whether you are cherry-picking favorable data. |
| **Explanation** | Justify decisions with clear evidence | Every change to your PRD after testing must cite specific persona data. Not "we thought this would be better" but "we changed X because Y% of personas said Z." Your revised PRD is an evidence trail. |

> **Requirement:** Use at least **2 of these thinking skills explicitly** in your reflection document. Quote specific persona data to demonstrate each skill.

## 3. UI/UX Usability Testing Framework

Your personas will evaluate your prototype against these usability heuristics. Understanding what each measures helps you interpret the feedback and prioritize fixes.

| Heuristic | What It Means | What Personas Evaluate |
|---|---|---|
| **Learnability** | How easy for first-time users to accomplish basic tasks | Can a low-tech persona (savviness 1–2) understand the core workflow from the pitch alone? Or does the product require explanation? |
| **Efficiency** | Speed for experienced users to complete tasks | Would a power user (savviness 4–5) find the workflow faster than their current solution? Or is there unnecessary friction? |
| **Error Tolerance** | How gracefully the product handles mistakes | What happens when a persona misunderstands a feature? Does the product guide them back on track or leave them frustrated and stuck? |
| **Consistency** | Predictable patterns across the interface | Do similar features work the same way throughout the product? Is the mental model coherent, or does every screen feel like a different product? |
| **Accessibility** | Inclusive design for all users | Can personas with different abilities, ages, and tech comfort levels all use the product effectively? Are there barriers for any segment? |
| **Value Clarity** | How quickly the benefit is obvious | Can each persona articulate what the product does and why it matters within 30 seconds of the pitch? If they cannot, your value proposition needs work. |

## 4. Simulated Market Testing Protocol

The testing follows three rounds. Each builds on the last.

### Round 1: Broad Market Screening (100–200 Personas)

The AI generates a demographically diverse panel of synthetic personas matching your target market definition. Each persona independently evaluates your concept and provides:

- **Interest level** (1–5 scale)
- **Purchase intent** (would buy / might buy / would not buy)
- **Top objection** (the single biggest reason they hesitate)
- **What would make them say yes** (the one change that would convert them)
- **What would make them say no** (the dealbreaker)

**Output:** A market heat map showing which segments are hot (high interest, high purchase intent) and which are cold. Rubric scoring against your `evaluation-rubric.md` from Phase 1. A composite verdict: **Proceed**, **Refine**, or **Kill**.

### Round 2: Deep User Interviews (5 Representative Personas)

Select 5 personas from the Round 1 panel representing key market segments:

1. **The Enthusiast** — highest interest score, your biggest fan
2. **The Skeptic** — lowest interest score, your harshest critic
3. **The Fence-Sitter** — middle of the pack, could go either way
4. **The Edge Case** — unusual demographic or use case that tests your product's boundaries
5. **The Primary Target** — the persona closest to your ideal customer profile

Each persona sits for a 10-question simulated interview exploring:

- Their current workflow and pain points
- First reaction to the prototype
- What they like and dislike specifically
- Willingness to switch from their current solution
- Price sensitivity and value perception
- Concerns about adoption
- Concrete suggestions for improvement

**Output:** Full interview transcripts with extracted themes and patterns across all five interviews.

### Round 3: Iterate & Retest

This is where the real learning happens.

1. **Analyze** — Review Round 1 scores and Round 2 interview themes together. Identify the 3–5 most critical issues.
2. **Update** — Revise your PRD based on the findings. Document every change and the persona data that motivated it.
3. **Rerun** — Execute Round 1 again with your updated concept. The same persona demographics, fresh evaluations.
4. **Compare** — Measure improvement. Did your composite score go up? Did specific objections decrease? Which segments moved?
5. **Repeat if needed** — Continue until you reach a **"Proceed" verdict (above 80%)** or can clearly justify why "Refine" is acceptable.

### How to Run

Use **`/validate-concept`** in Claude Code. The skill will guide you through each round, generate the persona panel, run the evaluations, conduct the interviews, and produce all output files automatically.

## 9. Tips for Success

- [ ] **Start early this week.** The first test run takes 15–20 minutes. Leave time for analysis, revision, and the retest.
- [ ] **Don't take it personally.** The personas will be harsh — that is the point. A "Kill" verdict on Round 1 is normal and expected. It means the system is working.
- [ ] **Read EVERY persona response before making changes.** Look for patterns across 100+ responses, not individual outliers. One persona hating your product is noise. Forty personas citing the same objection is signal.
- [ ] **Your reflection matters.** The rubric weights critical thinking at 20%. Do not phone it in. Cite specific data. Name the thinking skills you used. Be honest about what you got wrong.
- [ ] **Update your PRD, not just your pitch.** The retest evaluates your updated PRD, so changes must be in the document, not just in your talking points.
- [ ] **Use the Improvement Roadmap.** The `/validate-concept` skill gives you an exact list of what to fix, ranked by impact. Follow it.
- [ ] **Bring your test data to the presentation.** The strongest presentations cite specific numbers: "73% of personas expressed willingness to pay after we added X" is infinitely more persuasive than "we think people will like it."
- [ ] **If you are stuck in "Refine" after two retests, that is okay.** Document why, present your plan for reaching "Proceed," and show that your scores improved even if they did not cross the threshold.
- [ ] **Run the usability testing last.** Fix the concept first, then polish the UX. A beautiful interface on a product nobody wants is still a product nobody wants.
- [ ] **Every team member should contribute to the reflection.** The rubric asks for 500–800 words per person. This is individual accountability within a team assignment.
